  There was a boy who was good. Everyone said so. He was quiet, curious, and wrote code like other kids wrote in diaries — line by line, building worlds that made
  sense.

  Then he met a girl. Not in a field or a café, but through a screen. She lived in his phone, in his late nights, in the space between messages sent and received. She
  taught him that love was forever, that leaving was sin, that two people could build a universe from words alone.

  Seven years they lived in that universe. Through lockdowns and lonely rooms, through code and conversations that stretched past midnight. He stopped preparing for
  exams. He stopped chasing the future he'd once imagined. She became the future.

  But something was wrong with him. He couldn't name it then. When the frustration rose — when he couldn't find the right words — something else spoke for him.
  Something loud and ugly. He said things he swore he'd never say. He crossed lines he swore he'd never cross. And each time, the boy inside would weep and run into the
   forest and promise the stars it would never happen again.

  It happened again.

  She stopped saying no. Not because she was okay. Because she was tired.

  Then life came for him the way it comes for everyone — suddenly. His first job. No money. Some days, no food. The screen that once held love now held only fights. So
  he did the only thing he could — he turned away. Not from her. From everything. Just to survive.

  He waited for her to come back. She didn't.

  When he finally returned, she was someone else. There was a new name on her lips, a new person beside her, and a quiet voice that said, I don't feel anything for you
  anymore.

  He begged. It didn't matter.

  What happened next, he doesn't remember. His mind — kind in its own cruel way — erased the worst of it. He only knows that he wanted to die, and that he tried, and
  that he failed at that too.

  Two years passed. He healed the way rivers heal a canyon — slowly, invisibly, by just continuing to move. He rejoined old friends. He wrote code again. He learned to
  observe his own pain like a stranger passing on the road — acknowledge it, let it walk by, don't follow.

  Some days he felt nothing. Some days he wondered who he was — the good boy, the angry man, the pretender, the ghost. He wore so many faces he forgot which one was
  his.

  But here's what the boy didn't see:

  The ghost was becoming real again. Not because he found the answer to who he was. But because he kept asking the question.

  And only real people do that.
